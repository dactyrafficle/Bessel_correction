Bessel correction

Biased estimates of sample variances from a standard normal distribution N(0,1) w sample size n

The theoretical variance, σ2, of N(0,1) is 1.

But when you don't know the distribution, the best you can do is draw samples from that distribution and try to estimate the variance.

To estiamte the variance, s2, take a sample of size n from N(0,1), and calculate s2 = (1/n-1) Σ (x-x̄)2.

The (n-1) bit is confusing, so let's look at that.

If you use (1/n), you'll systematically underestimate the variance because the mean you calculate, x̄, will be closer to the realizations in a given sample than the theoretical mean, μ. So the sum of squares Σ (x-x̄)2 is downward biased. Nerds proved this mathematically a long time ago.

This animation shows that. We're taking 0 samples of sample size n, calculating s2 for each sample, then taking the average to make s2.
